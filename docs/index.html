How this works

This tool implements the odor-classification models from Mayhew et al., “Transport features predict if a molecule is odorous,” PNAS 2022. The central idea: a molecule smells only if it can physically complete the journey to an olfactory receptor. That journey is governed by simple transport properties — volatility (vapor pressure / boiling point) and hydrophobicity (logP). Molecules that are too heavy or too polar never reach the receptors and are odorless.

Two models from the paper are used here:

  • Rule of three — a molecule with molecular weight between 30 and 300 Da and fewer than four heteroatoms is generally odorous. Works for any structure and is computed entirely in your browser. In the paper, 96% of molecules meeting these criteria are odorous.
  • Transport boundary model — logistic-regression boundaries in vapor pressure / logP space (AUROC ≈ 0.88–0.98). Requires a vapor-pressure value.

When you enter one of the 1,924 molecules from the study's curated dataset, the tool also shows the actual gradient-boosted transport-ML probability reported in the paper. Structure parsing, 2-D depiction, molecular weight, logP and heteroatom counts are computed client-side with RDKit (WebAssembly). Chemical-name lookup uses the public PubChem API.

For research and educational use. Predictions are estimates of odor class, not odor quality or intensity, and can be wrong — especially for molecules unlike those in the training set.

Model & data: Mayhew, Arayata, Gerkin, Lee, Magill, Snyder, Little, Yu & Mainland, PNAS 119(15):e2116576119 (2022). Code & dataset: github.com/emayhew/OlfactorySpace.
